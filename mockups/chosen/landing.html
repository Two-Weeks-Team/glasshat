§2 · How it happens

A judge of judges. In three acts.

Act I · 01

The Six Hats read the work.

Pitch deck + GitHub repo go in. Six perspective agents — White (facts), Red (intuition), Yellow (value), Black (risk), Green (alternatives), Blue (synthesis) — score in parallel under the rubric the evaluator actually uses.

Act I · 02

Yellow gets too excited.

The optimist hat scores A1 = 9.0/10. But the evidence depth signal is only 0.31 — well below threshold. Three independent detectors fire: Phoenix Online Eval, a Python statistical custom evaluator, and Black hat's counter-claim.

Act II · 03

Phoenix opens its eye.

The orchestrator pauses. It calls Phoenix MCP — get-experiment-by-id, get-span-annotations, get-dataset-examples — and Qdrant.recommend with positive anchors (correctly-calibrated past Yellow A1 spans) and negative anchors (over-confident ones). Four tool calls return in under 800ms.

Act II · 04

Three past winners humble the optimist.

The vector space returns three past projects with similar evidence-depth profiles — all scored 7.2 / 7.5 / 7.8 on A1. The score calibration agent applies clip(9.0 − 0.8 × 1.75, p25=6.8, p75=8.1) = 7.6 with a ±2.0 cap. Loop exits with escalate=True.

Act III · 05

The 3D constellation reshapes.

503 past anchor projects sit in a 3D space (UMAP of 768d Gemini embeddings). The current submission's point — magenta — was drifting toward the winner cluster (gold). After correction, it slides toward the "overhyped, undelivered" cluster. The vector space tells the truth.

Act III · 06

Same submission. Two rubrics. Two scores.

Glasshat runs the BMAD scorer twice — once under Qdrant's 3-axis rubric (Functionality / Originality / UX) and once under Rapid Agent's 4-axis (Tech 40 / Inn 30 / Imp 20 / Pres 10, with Tech tie-break). The same project lands at 87 and 73. That's not bias. That's rubric-faithful variance.
